## ✅ What Earns It the B (Not Lower)

### 1. World-Building and Lore — Genuinely Excellent
The Venetian-inspired city of Ombre is one of the most thoughtfully constructed settings in indie RPGs. The game has lore in spades, with everything feeling well thought out. The class conflict between the Masquerada and the Contadani gives the story real political weight — not just fantasy window dressing.

### 2. Writing and Characters — Above Average
Masquerada is the rare RPG that really sticks with the player after the credits roll. Rather than dealing with trope-filled characters, every member of the cast feels like a real person, making their actions seem much more believable when they're forced to deal with truly terrible situations.

### 3. Voice Acting — Exceptional for an Indie
Jennifer Hale, Michelle Lukes, and Matthew Mercer headline a professional voice actor team that gives the game an extra touch of quality that rarely happens in indie games. Every single line is voiced — a massive production commitment for a 15-person studio.

### 4. Art Style — Distinctive and Gorgeous
Everything from the comic book art style to the way faces are fully animated in banners is positively delightful. The isometric cel-shaded style evokes its Renaissance/Venetian-inspired setting beautifully.

---

## ❌ Why It Doesn't Reach A or S

### 1. Combat Is the Game's Achilles' Heel
This is the big one. Even though the story and presentation are excellent, the gameplay is unfortunately very monotonous. Merely holding the attack button and unleashing the occasional ability is more than enough to overcome the majority of opponents. For a game billing itself as a **tactical RPG**, this is a serious problem.

### 2. Extreme Linearity
Masquerada is very linear throughout — the vast majority of the time you're just running through corridors. In lacking the usual RPG fare such as equipment, meaningful decisions, sidequests, and NPC interaction, it offers very little to players who care about gameplay mechanics.

### 3. Story and Gameplay Don't Mesh
While neither the story nor battle system is particularly bad, the way they come together leaves an unwelcome void. The game essentially splits into two separate experiences: a near-visual-novel story mode, and shallow combat sequences — neither fully feeding the other.

### 4. No Meaningful Progression
There is almost no character growth. No real leveling up, no equipment system. For an RPG, that's a serious structural omission that makes the "RPG" label feel somewhat misleading.

### 5. Low Replayability
Once the credits roll, there is very little reason to return to Ombre. The game is a single satisfying playthrough — and that's it.

---

## 🏁 Final Verdict

Masquerada: Songs and Shadows is a **beautiful, heartfelt game wearing an RPG mask** — but under that mask, the gameplay is closer to a visual novel with combat interludes. If you love story above all else, it might personally feel like an **A**. But as a complete package judged on all pillars of game design, **B Tier** is where it rightfully sits.

> **Who should play it:** Story-first RPG fans, visual novel enthusiasts, anyone who loves rich world-building and top-tier voice acting.
>
> **Who should skip it:** Players looking for deep tactical combat, meaningful progression systems, or high replayability.
